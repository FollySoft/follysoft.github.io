What comes to mind when you hear the word “play”? Do you imagine someone playing a guitar? Do you see children playing hide and seek in the park? Do you imagine actors, performing roles in a play on Broadway?

Something I always like to do when I’m exploring a particular subject is look up the dictionary definition of a word, as a sort of springboard for the conversation. Merriam Webster’s dictionary splits the word play up into it’s noun and verb forms, and the definitions of each are extensive, featuring numerous subcategories. Per this dictionary, the noun form of the word “play” might be considered as a “a particular act or maneuver in a game”, or an act of “amorous flirtation”, or the even broader concept of “recreational activity”, among others. The verb form can be defined as “to take advantage”, “to behave or conduct oneself in a specified way” (i.e. “play it safe”), or “to pretend to engage in the activities of,” once more, among many others.

Some scholars of play try their best to encapsulate all of these into one singular definition of the word. My own favorite, and the one I feel is the most encapsulating, comes from Katie Salen: “Play is free movement within a more rigid structure.” Other brilliant minds, particularly in the sphere of game design, have attempted to grasp play in a similar manner: Johan Huizinga, Jesse Schell, Ian Bogost, and Catherine Garvey are only a few whom I’ve managed to find such encompassing quotes from.

Yet we encounter a new difficulty when trying to relate this idea of “play” into Christian beliefs and theology, particularly when attempting to find it’s reference in scripture. Using biblegateway.com, we can find several references to the word “play” that appear in the NIV translation of the bible, but they are mostly limited to the playing of instruments (which does, of course, still count for our purposes). Exploring other translations, we do find the word “play” used in some broader contexts, though used in a more negative tone. For example, The Message has Moses saying the phrase “don’t play games with us” (Exodus 8:29) when speaking with Pharaoh. Deuteronomy also has a few references of the phrase “playing favorites” (Deuteronomy 1:16-17, 10:14-18, 16:18-19).

It seems that these broader applications of “play” that we have come to understand today don’t really have much of a place in scripture, as long as we’re searching for the word itself. Searching biblegateway.com for synonyms of the word “play” (“leisure” and “entertainment” for example) also produce limited results. However, we did already identify that the word “play” is already a difficult word to define, even in our modern age. Perhaps there are other ways that scripture discusses what we now understand as play and leisure?
