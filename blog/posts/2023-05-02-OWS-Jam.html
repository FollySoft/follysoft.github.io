This is my inargural blog post on my new website! I'm unsure how frequently I'll be making posts, but with the unhealthy state of social media platforms lately, I figured it was a good idea to try and start this up. I'm starting off by chatting a little bit about the first Open World Shutter Jam - a jam centered on the usage of the Open World Shutter software by Jonny Hopkins. Below you'll find a dump of shots I managed to capture during the span on the jam, many of which were unsubmitted. I've had so much fun with this lately that I had to try and limit myself on the number of entries I submitted for the jam...

I had dabbled with the tool a little bit in the past when it was first released. I have been following Jonny's work for a while - his work in the alt-ctrl games scene has been particularly inspirational to much of my own work. When this tool was released, I was knee-deep in Elden Ring, and the games beautifully erie aesthetic, paired the ability to diable the games HUD and use of the in-game telescope, it felt like the perfect place to test the waters. Despite my love of photography as a hobby, I think this may have been the closest I've ever really come to actually playing with any sort of camera-related mode or tools in (or with) a game.

I have a diffuclt time participating in jams these days. Even lengthier jams can still result in me burning out on game development for weeks at a time until I can find the energy and motivation to open up my tools again. However, seeing Jonny announce a jam for the Open World Shutter, along side of it's recent submission into the 2023 A MAZE Festival in Berlin, I simply had to participate and take the opportunity to play around with this wonderful tool even more.

The jam's theme was "Synthesis", and I had a couple of ideas that tried to break out of the mold of strictly video games and using the camera with other software:

Unfornately, past this point, I ran out of ideas that even loosely fit the jam theme, and just went whole hog into trying out a variety of games and moving imagery:

I would later discover that one of my favorite games to photograph was Sea of thieves, with it's bright colors and relatively minimal UI (which can also be disabled). In particular, downing a mug of grog and looking at things while your vision began to bend produced some of my favorite images:

From this point I went back to exploring games in my library that had interesting and unique perspectives or object movement, with bonus points for colorful artwork and aesthetics:

It took me far too long to realize that No Man's Sky would be a perfect candidate for this tool. Even better once I discovered that not only could you disable the HUD, but also completely turn off any head bobbing or screenshaking:
