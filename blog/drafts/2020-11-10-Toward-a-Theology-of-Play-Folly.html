I started this draft this morning with the title "Toward a Theology of Play: Folly", while Discord is open on the monitor to my left. In it, a member of the CGDC community has begun a poll with the title "WHY DO YOU WANT TO MAKE VIDEO GAMES?" listing 5 different options:

  • I love to be creative!! (currently 4 votes)
  • I want to make a career out of it!! (currently 1 vote)
  • I thrive on seeing a project finished. (currently 1 vote)
  • I've always had this idea in my head... (currently 3 votes)
  • [Other] (currently 1 vote)

The reason I bring this up, however, is that a member of the community responded with this specific message, emphasis my own:

Over the last decade I've worked in game dev I would have voted for each of those options at different times in life. Ultimately they're all folly if it isn't to serve a higher purpose and calling.
The fact that these words were spoken, in this context, in the hours after I created this document with "Folly" within the title, felt like a sign that I needed to continue forward with discussing this topic, and the term "folly" in partuclar, within the contexts of creativity and Chritian theology.

Recently, I changed a lot of my online handles to 'FollySoft'. This word has held so much of my interest in the years following my initial desire to make games that I feel it's come to define my very creative practice. Once before, I attempted to write a manifesto around the idea of "creative folly" with the assistance of a close friend who had also been thinking on the subject. Yet I never really felt like it quite said what I hoped it would say. So I feel it's time I looked at it from the biblical perspective in hopes that I can truely get a grasp of the paradoxical feelings we all have toward this word.

An easy place to start is the bible. Fools and folly are, by and large, not depicted in a favorable light in scripture, and rightfully so.
