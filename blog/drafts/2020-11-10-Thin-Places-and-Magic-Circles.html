In my previous post I had mentioned that my church recently celebrated Ash Wednesday to mark the beginning of Lent. I've come to consider lent as a time to recenter my focus on my own faith and beliefs, to better make sense of some things or try to answer some questions that I have yet to really resolve. So I'm making an effort to continue on with blogging about faith and play, in hopes that somehow I can resolve the mysterious connection between these two in my own head.

I had recently been reading through an essay found in "The Legend of Zelda and Theology" and came across a concept that I felt the need to jot down. In particular, the essay titled "Portals, Prophecy, and Cuccos: Considerations of Power in a Link to the Past", by Jeremy Smith, touches on the idea of "thin places". When talking about the portals that the player may use to ravel between the light and dark worlds of ALttP, Smith briefly explains the core concept and history of "thin places" in theology. Smith writes, "perhaps real-life portals are 'thin places' where the divine world and our tangible world meet or are even seen more clearly... they are places where our light/dark sides are most intimately connected". Citing the Trappist monk Thomas Melton, he writes "God shows [God's] self in everything - in people and in things and in nature and events". Smith expands on this further by referencing the writing of Theologian Marcus Borg: "Music, poetry, literature, the visual arts, and dance, can all become thin places in which the boundary between one's self and the world momentarily disappears" (emphasis mine).

Those last few words in Borg's talk of "thin places" is what has led me to writing this blog post, as I can't help but see some similar language in the above quotations being reflected in some of my recent academic readings of "play".
